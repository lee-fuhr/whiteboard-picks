How to choose the right whiteboard

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

For most buyers in 2026, a porcelain-on-steel magnetic whiteboard offers the best combination of durability, erase-ability, and value; but if your use is light or your budget is tight, a high-quality melamine board will serve you well if you accept it will wear faster.

What whiteboard surface is right for you?

The surface material is the most important decision. Melamine boards are the most budget-friendly option, made from a resin-coated hardboard. They work fine for occasional use in a home office or dorm room, but they ghost and stain over time and are not magnetic unless specified. Expect to replace one after a few years of steady use. Porcelain-on-steel boards are the industry standard for schools and corporate settings. The baked-on enamel resists staining, erases cleanly for decades, and the steel core makes every inch magnetic. They are heavier and cost more up front, but you will likely never have to buy another board. Glass whiteboards are a premium choice with a sleek, frameless look. Tempered glass is non-porous so it never ghosts, and it is magnetic if backed with steel. The trade-offs are a higher price, noticeable glare under bright lights, and a reflective surface that can be distracting in some rooms. You also need to mount them carefully to avoid shattering during installation.

Do you need a magnetic whiteboard?

For virtually all group work and classroom environments, a magnetic surface is a must. It lets you pin up handouts, sticky notes, printed agendas, or even lightweight tools with magnets. Without magnetism you lose a whole dimension of interactivity. Unless you know you will only ever write on it, choose magnetic. If the board is for personal checklists or reminders that you never need to tack things to, a non-magnetic board will save you some money. But even then, many people end up wishing they had the option later. The extra convenience is usually worth the small premium.

How to pick the right size and shape

Size should be driven by your room dimensions and the distance from the farthest viewer. A common rule from office designers is that the whiteboard should be at least one-sixth the width of the wall it hangs on. For a small meeting room seating four to six, a board in the range of 3 feet by 4 feet works well. For a classroom or large conference room, go up to 4 feet by 6 feet or wider. Consider the layout: horizontal (landscape) boards fit most standard walls and allow several people to write side by side. Vertical (portrait) boards are great for narrow spaces like hallways or between windows. Some boards come in square shapes or custom sizes, but those are harder to find and usually must be special ordered. Stick with common aspect ratios unless you have a specific need.

Framed versus frameless: which is better?

Framed whiteboards include an aluminum or plastic border that holds the surface rigid and often includes a marker tray. The frame protects the edges from chipping and gives the board a finished look. These are the most common and usually the most affordable. The only downside is the frame can catch dust and some people prefer the cleaner aesthetic of a frameless board. Frameless boards (often glass or thin steel with edge-to-edge writing surface) look modern and integrate seamlessly into minimalist decor. However, they are generally more expensive to manufacture and install. The lack of a frame means the board must be glued or mounted with special hardware to prevent the glass from shifting. If you value clean lines and don’t mind the higher cost and careful installation, go frameless.

Mounting options: fixed, mobile, or portable?

A fixed wall mount is the most stable and professional choice. Most boards come with a standard mounting bracket system that attaches to wall studs or drywall anchors. Make sure you have the right tools and know your wall type, heavy porcelain boards need solid anchors. If you rent or frequently rearrange furniture, consider an easel-style board on wheels. These are typically lighter and can be moved from room to room, though they may wobble during vigorous writing. Portable whiteboards (flip chart style or thin flexible boards that roll up) are an option for temporary use, like workshops or trade shows. They are not as durable and the writing surface can be uneven. For a permanent workspace, invest in a fixed mount or a sturdy rolling stand. Always check weight limits and mounting hardware quality before buying.

Maintenance and marker compatibility

Use only low-odor dry-erase markers. Expo and generic brands both work, but avoid permanent markers, if someone uses one by accident, you can often remove it by tracing over with a dry-erase marker and wiping quickly. For daily cleaning, a soft dry cloth or microfiber eraser is enough. Avoid abrasive cleaners that scratch melamine or leave residue on glass. Ghosting (faint outlines of old writing) happens on all boards eventually, but faster on melamine. To minimize it, clean the board regularly with a dedicated whiteboard cleaner or a mild solution of water and isopropyl alcohol. For porcelain boards, ghosting is rare and usually comes off with the right cleaner. Glass boards virtually never ghost because the surface is non-porous. A good rule of thumb: if you want zero maintenance hassle, glass or porcelain are worth the investment.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use any marker on a whiteboard?

Stick to dry-erase markers made for whiteboards. Permanent markers can be removed if caught quickly by tracing over with a dry-erase marker, but they can permanently stain melamine surfaces. Never use wet-erase or chalk markers unless the board is specifically labeled for them.

How do I remove ghosting and stains?

For mild ghosting, wipe with a microfiber cloth and isopropyl alcohol or a dedicated whiteboard cleaner. For stubborn stains on porcelain or glass, a paste of baking soda and water can be gently rubbed. Avoid acetone or harsh solvents on melamine boards as they can damage the coating.

Are glass whiteboards worth the higher price?

If you value a completely ghost-free surface, modern aesthetics, and easy cleaning, glass boards are excellent. They also offer a very smooth writing experience. However, they are heavier, more expensive, and can show glare. For a home office or design-focused meeting room, they are a good choice; for heavy daily use in a school, a porcelain board is more practical and durable.

What size whiteboard do I need for a typical meeting room?

For a small room seating 4–6 people, a board around 3 feet by 4 feet is sufficient. For larger rooms, go up to 4 feet by 6 feet or wider. The general guideline is to have the board width equal to about one-sixth of the wall length to maintain visual balance and readability from the back of the room.

Do I need a magnetic whiteboard?

Unless you are absolutely sure you will never need to pin up papers, photos, or notes, get a magnetic board. The ability to attach materials with magnets adds huge versatility for meetings, teaching, and planning. Non-magnetic boards are slightly cheaper but limit what you can do with the surface.

How do I mount a whiteboard to drywall without studs?

Use heavy-duty drywall anchors rated to hold the board’s weight. For porcelain or glass boards, which can be very heavy, it is best to locate at least one or two studs. If you cannot find a stud, use toggle bolts designed for hollow walls. Always follow the mounting instructions that come with the board and double-check weight ratings.

In shortThe best whiteboard for you comes down to three simple questions: how often will you use it, do you need magnets, and what is your wall situation. A porcelain-on-steel board hits the sweet spot for most people and will last years without losing performance. If your budget is tighter or your writing needs are light, a melamine board will get the job done. Buy a size that fits your space and a surface that matches your maintenance tolerance, and you will have a tool that makes meetings, lessons, and planning sessions noticeably more productive.
